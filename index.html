The bear case

Why sober investors say $2T is a bubble.

Every big narrative gets the treatment it deserves. Here's the case the shorts and value investors are making — grounded in the S-1 prospectus, historical IPO data (Prof. Jay Ritter, "Mr. IPO"), and the market's own signals over the last three weeks.

Bear 01
115×

Astronomical P/S multiple

Trailing price-to-sales of 115.66× is essentially unprecedented for a company this large. Nasdaq median is ~5×. Even Tesla at peak euphoria was 30×. Forward P/S at aggressive growth is still 84×. Sales, not earnings.

Bear 02
−$2.58B

GAAP unprofitable

Consolidated operating loss. Ritter's academic dataset (9,253 IPOs since 1980) shows unprofitable issuers underperform the market by −30.7% over 3 years. Not zero return — actively worse than the index.

Bear 03
−$7B / yr

Two of three segments burn cash

Space loses $657M on $4B revenue. AI (Grok) loses $6.35B on $3.2B revenue — a −199% operating margin. Starlink's $4.4B profit funds both, leaving nothing for shareholders.

Bear 04
$28.5T

The fabricated TAM

The underwriter deck's Total Addressable Market claim is $28.5T — literally 90% of US GDP ($31.8T) and ~24% of global GDP. 93% of that is attributed to the AI segment (the worst performer). Textbook narrative inflation.

Bear 05
$300M+

Bond market flashing yellow

SpaceX's inaugural bond issuance has posted secondary-market losses exceeding $300M. Debt investors — who look at cash flows, not narratives — are pricing in higher risk than the equity crowd.

Bear 06
96% → 50%

Starlink growth is decelerating

The engine of the story grew revenue 96% in 2024 but only 50% in 2025. Classic S-curve, and it's the segment carrying the entire valuation. If growth drops to 30% in 2026, the base-case model in this app collapses.

Bear 07
−7.80%

Off the highs, already

Down 7.80% today alone. 52-week high was $225.64 in early euphoria; now at $157. Off ~30% from the peak in ~3 weeks. The post-IPO honeymoon shortened faster than most.

Bear 08
Key-person

Concentration on one CEO

Musk runs SpaceX, xAI, Tesla, X, and Neuralink simultaneously. Retention of top engineers, political entanglement, and reputational tail risks are real. Post-Twitter drama has already cost xAI (Grok) meaningful advertiser dollars.

Bear 09
Regulatory

Spectrum & approvals

Direct-to-cell relies on cellular spectrum coordination with 100+ national regulators. Starship launches face FAA + state environmental review. AT&T is already pushing back at the FCC. Not a physics risk — a policy risk.

The bear's punchline. Buffett has always avoided IPOs because "the seller chooses when to come public — you're almost certainly buying at their optimal price, not yours." Munger called sell-side forecasts "total nonsense" and warned against the availability bias of remembering Amazon and Google while forgetting the 90% of listings that lost most of their value. SpaceX at 115× sales is being priced as if execution is guaranteed. History says otherwise.
The bet in one sentence

The physics is solved. The engineering isn't easy.

Here's the honest framing. Nothing in SpaceX's plan requires new physics — no dark matter propulsion, no fusion breakthrough, no room-temperature superconductor. Everything below has been done somewhere, by someone. The bet is whether SpaceX can do all of it, at the same time, at unprecedented cadence, at unprecedented cost. That's not "just execute." That's a systems-integration problem the size of Apollo — which took 400,000 people, $257B (2026 dollars), and lost 3 astronauts along the way.

Solved physics — the science is done

These principles are 50–120 years old
  • Rocket equation. Tsiolkovsky, 1903. Given exhaust velocity + mass ratio, you get delta-V. Full stop.
  • Orbital mechanics. Kepler + Newton, 1600s–1700s. Every trajectory is a solved differential equation.
  • Chemical propulsion. Methalox (methane + LOX) has flown before. Raptor is a refinement, not a breakthrough.
  • Retro-propulsive landing. Falcon 9 proved this in 2015. Booster catch is refinement of proven physics.
  • Reentry thermal protection. Space Shuttle tiles (1981), Dragon PICA-X (2010). Vacuum-facing radiators (ISS).
  • Stainless steel airframe. Convair Atlas used balloon-tank stainless in the 1950s. It's a mature material.
  • Phased-array antennas. Radar tech since WWII. Starlink terminal is commodity manufacturing.
  • Inter-satellite laser links. Already flying on ~4,000 Starlink satellites at ~200 Gbps.
  • Radiative cooling in vacuum. ISS radiators dump ~70 kW. Scaling is engineering, not physics.
  • Solar power in orbit. Every satellite since Sputnik-3 (1958). ~1.4 kW/m², no night in most LEO passes.

Open engineering — the plan needs to work

Each is achievable; all-at-once at cadence is the hard part
  • Ship-side full reuse. Super Heavy already reuses — Flight 9 was the first reused booster (B14-2), Flight 11 was the second flight of B15. But the ship has never been caught: all 12 flights have splashed in the Indian Ocean. Musk: "we'll only try to catch ship after two perfect soft landings." Flight 14 is the earliest catch attempt.
  • Ship reliability under reentry. As of Flight 12, ship is 6 controlled ocean landings out of 10 attempts (60%). Block 2 regressed on Flights 7–9 before recovering on 10–11. Block 3 debuted successfully on the ship side in May 2026. Improving — not solved.
  • Cadence at 100+ flights/year. Not a physics limit — Falcon 9 already flew 138 times in 2024. Global aviation does 100,000 flights/day. Starship itself did 5 flights in 2025 and 1 in 2026 through July — the bottleneck is Raptor production, pad turnaround (~30 days, target 3), and FAA licensing. SpaceX's Starfactory is designed for 1,000 Starships/year.
  • $60/kg cost target. Currently more like $500–1,000/kg. Requires Raptor unit cost decline, factory learning curve, cheap refurb — all unproven at Starship scale but every step is engineering + operations, not physics.
  • Orbital refueling. Ship-to-ship propellant transfer at cryogenic temperatures. NASA's HLS demo target 2026–27 with a depot + tanker pair. Never done at operational scale; physics is trivially known — hardware qualification is the work.
  • V3 satellite production. Flight 12 deployed 2 modified V3s — the first real ones to fly. Manufacturing 40,000+ V3 satellites at $600K each is another factory build-out. AI1 payloads add thermal + compute complexity on top.
  • V3 dispenser at cadence. Flight 12 demonstrated actual V3 deployment (not just mock). Full operational deployment of 60+ V3s per Starship not yet proven — robotics + timing problem, tractable.
  • Direct-to-cell coverage. Currently ~500 satellites. Consumer expectation is 5-bar coverage; delivery has been intermittent. Fixes with more sats + more spectrum — both are money and time, not invention.
  • Grok / xAI monetization. Nothing physical here — a business execution question. Losing $6.35B/yr against ChatGPT + Gemini + Claude is not a fixable "just launch harder" problem.
  • Regulatory throughput. Starship is currently grounded pending FAA acceptance of the Flight 12 booster investigation. FAA licensing + state environmental review + international spectrum coordination are all bottleneck-capable.
  • Political tail risks. Musk's political entanglement + concentration risk on one CEO. Executable via succession, but currently unmitigated.
The honest framing. Every open engineering problem above is a throughput + operations + capital problem, not a physics problem. Falcon 9 hitting 138 flights/year (2024) proves SpaceX can operate at industrial cadence when the vehicle is mature. Global aviation moves 100,000 planes per day — 100 rockets per year is trivially achievable in absolute terms. Even expendable Starship at 100/year is just an industrial-throughput question: a single 777 assembly line does more than that, and SpaceX's Starfactory is designed for 1,000 Starships per year. Reusability makes it cheaper and faster, not possible/impossible. So the honest framing: the physics is solved, the hardware is proven at Falcon scale, and Starship is the same team applying the same playbook to a bigger vehicle. The bet is whether they can carry that discipline through 5 more years of scale-up without losing focus, pad, or key people. Reasonable people put that probability anywhere from 50% (bear) to 95% (bull).
The story so far

Three eras of getting bits to space.

Every era in orbital communications has been defined by the launch cost curve underneath it. The Shuttle-era priced constellations out of existence. Falcon 9 made Starlink possible. Starship rewrites the rules again.

Era 1 · Pre-2015

The Shuttle / Expendable era

~$65/Mbps
Bandwidth cost to LEO

Expendable rockets at ~$10K/kg and GEO comsats at <100 Mbps/kg. Constellations were financially impossible outside government-scale defense contracts. Iridium filed for bankruptcy in 1999 for a reason.

Era 2 · 2015–Present

Falcon 9 + Starlink V2 Mini

$6.55/Mbps
Bandwidth cost to LEO

Reusable Falcon 9 at ~$1,000/kg. V2 Mini satellites at ~150–170 Mbps/kg. A ~10× improvement on the prior regime — enough to make Starlink real. 12M+ subscribers as of mid-2026, still doubling annually. First profitable satellite broadband ever.

Era 3 · 2026+

Starship + V3Flying

$0.30/Mbps
Bandwidth cost to LEO

Starship at <$185/kg with 100–200 t payload. V3 satellites at 500–600 Mbps/kg (4× the density of V2 Mini). Combined: another ~20× improvement. Flight 12 (May 2026) was the Block 3 debut and the first flight to deploy actual modified V3 satellites — 20 simulators + 2 real V3s from Pad-2. Ship recovered cleanly; booster stumbled on boostback (20/33 engines lit) and is under FAA review. Flight 13 targeted for July 2026.

Deployment cost per Gbps served — head to head

Terrestrial cost is per-premise (a fixed sunk cost regardless of usage). Satellite cost is per Gbps of orbital capacity (spread across many customers, plus ground network). Different denominators, same conclusion: satellite economics finally beat fiber for low-density markets.
Why this matters. Comcast can't profitably lay fiber to a farm in Kansas — the $2,000/premise capex takes 20 years to recover at $60/mo rural pricing. Starlink can serve that same farm for ~$3 in amortized launch cost per year of service. The economics of terrestrial ISPs are set by density. The economics of Starlink are set by launch cost per Gbps. Starship inverts which one wins in the 60% of the planet where fiber can't reach.

The second act

Space is the new data center location.

The same launch cost curve that unlocks Starlink also unlocks something bigger: orbital compute. Starcloud put an NVIDIA H100 in orbit in November 2025 on a demo mission. Google announced Project Suncatcher — a TPU constellation — that same month. SpaceX is building AI-optimized satellites (widely reported as "AI1") on the Starlink V3 bus, co-developed with NVIDIA, targeting 2027 launches. In space you get free 24/7 solar, unlimited radiative cooling, and no permitting fights. When launch is $60/kg, the math flips.

Nov 2025 · flew

Starcloud-1 — first H100 in orbit

Startup Starcloud (formerly Lumen Orbit) launched an NVIDIA H100 GPU to LEO on a rideshare mission, running training workloads in-orbit and downlinking results. Proof point that datacenter-class silicon survives launch loads and can be actively cooled by radiators.

Nov 2025 · announced

Google Project Suncatcher — TPU constellation

Google unveiled plans for a constellation of TPU-carrying satellites connected by inter-satellite laser links, framed as "solar-powered AI training in orbit." Direct competitor for the orbital-compute TAM SpaceX is aiming at.

May 2026 · flew (Block 3)

Starship Flight 12 — first V3s deployed

Starship Block 3 debut from Pad-2 at Starbase. Ship deployed 2 modified Starlink V3 satellites + 20 simulators (~44 t payload) and splashed down cleanly in the Indian Ocean. Booster failed on boostback (20/33 engines lit). FAA investigation gates Flight 13, targeted July 2026 — the payload path for AI1 satellites is now flight-proven.

2027 · in dev

Starlink AI1 (NVIDIA-partnered)

SpaceX's AI-optimized satellite variant on the V3 bus, co-developed with NVIDIA. Combines Starlink's inter-sat laser mesh (already flying) with rack-scale GPUs to make each Starship launch a mini datacenter delivery. Now that the V3 bus itself has flown on Flight 12, the AI1 payload path is closer to real.

Why compute in orbit actually works

☀️

Free power

Solar panels in LEO generate ~1.4 kW/m² with no atmosphere, no night for most orbits. Earth data centers spend 30–40% of TCO on grid power.

❄️

Free cooling

Radiative cooling into 2.7 K vacuum. No chillers, no water, no cooling towers. PUE approaches 1.0 vs terrestrial 1.5+ for AI racks.

⚡

Zero-latency mesh

Starlink already flies laser inter-sat links at ~200 Gbps per hop. Stack GPUs across dozens of satellites; treat the constellation as a single GPU pod.

🏛️

No permitting

No town-council fights over water use, no grid-interconnect queues, no gas turbine build-outs. Launch a rack, plug it into the mesh.

Compute per Starship — the rental economics

The business model is CoreWeave in orbit: SpaceX deploys GPU-loaded satellites and rents the compute out at $/GPU-hour, competing with AWS, Azure, and CoreWeave. The pricing floor is set by terrestrial data centers ($2–4/hr for an H100). SpaceX's cost floor is lower because power and cooling are free in orbit — so they can either undercut Earth prices or match them and take the margin. The business, in one sentence. Every Starship launch of AI1 satellites deploys ~$170M of GPU capex + $18.5M of launch cost. That fleet generates ~$100M/year in rental revenue at commodity pricing, pays itself back in under 2 years, and throws off ~$70M/year in gross profit for 3–8 more years. Do that 100 times a year and it's a $10B/year revenue line — and that's before the price premium orbital compute can charge for zero-carbon, physically-isolated, latency-tolerant training workloads. This is why "AI Compute · $600B Y5 EV" is on the hero tile, and why the sum-of-parts base case has $60B revenue by 2031. It's not vaporware; it's a rental business with real payback math.
The S-1 reversal — why today's "loss" flips to profit fast. The bear case above (Bear #2, Bear #3) leans on the S-1's −$2.58B consolidated operating loss and calls the AI segment a "capital incinerator." But re-read the loss line with the calculator above in view: SpaceX isn't burning $2.58B — it's spending it on GPU racks and launch infrastructure that pay themselves back in under 2 years. Every 20 AI1 launches per year adds a ~$2B annual revenue line — enough to erase the entire current consolidated operating loss with a single year of orbital compute deployment. Run 100 launches/year (well within Starfactory's 1,000/yr design capacity and Falcon 9's proven 138/yr cadence) and the AI segment stops being a "capital incinerator" and turns into a $10B+ revenue business at ~70% gross margin. That's Amazon 2000 or Netflix 2012 — an income-statement loss today masking a rental business whose payback math has already been proven at Earth data centers, now applied to a lower-cost operating environment. The S-1 shows the cost. The 2028 P&L will show the payoff.

What $0.10/Mbps unlocks.

At Starship maturity, $1B of launch spend deploys ~10,000 Tbps of new bandwidth — roughly 15× the entire Starlink constellation today. That's ~90 days of Starlink operating income at current scale. When launch stops being the constraint, the market opens up.

1

Rural + emerging markets

At $0.10/Mbps deployed, Starlink can profitably serve markets that today can't pay $90/mo. India, sub-Saharan Africa, LatAm rural. TAM doubles or triples.

2

Mobility & backhaul

Aviation, maritime, trucking, cellular backhaul, T-Mobile direct-to-cell. Every network operator becomes a Starlink customer for capacity augmentation.

3

Enterprise SLA tiers

Lower unit cost + narrower beams = premium tiers with guaranteed capacity. Compete directly with fiber for on-premise enterprise up-time.

4

Space-based AI compute

Cheap $/kg means you can loft rack-scale GPU clusters. Cool with radiators, power with solar, offload latency-tolerant training from data centers.

5

Starshield & defense

Custom LEO ISR & comms constellations for DoD. Each Starship launch places a full sub-constellation. Multi-billion-dollar sole-source contracts.

6

Cislunar & Mars logistics

When trucking to LEO is a rounding error, propellant depots, lunar cargo, and Mars supply pencil out. Optionality on the next century of trade routes.
